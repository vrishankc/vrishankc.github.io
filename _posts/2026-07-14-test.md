In just a week, I found myself in Scandinavia sharing the stage with Nobel Laureates, navigating conversations in seven different languages, ice skating for the first time, dancing to EDM until 5 A.M. with members of the Nobel Prize Committee, and having the privilege of attending one of the most prestigious ceremonies in the world.

In May 2025, I received an award that granted me a fully funded trip to present at the Stockholm International Youth Science Seminar (SIYSS) and attend the Nobel Prize Ceremonies and associated festivities. It was my first time in Europe, and I was excited about the people I would meet and places I would see, along with nervousness at the thought of meeting some of the world's leading scientists. 

With a quick goodbye to my roommate, I rushed to the Boston-Logan Airport to board my flight. Important context: I hate flying--so the turbulent initial flight to Frankfurt was horrible for my flight anxiety. As the plane shook, I was gripping onto my seat so hard that people near me had to ask if I was doing okay. Upon (thankfully) reaching Frankfurt in one frazzled piece, I met one of the fellow prize winners, Aarushi, from Johns Hopkins University. We boarded our connecting flight to Stockholm together, and surprisingly, the final leg of the journey was altogether less turbulent than I expected.

Upon landing and traveling to a hotel with an unexpectedly jovial Uber driver who I spontaneously sang Hindi songs with, we were greeted by our hosts--Paulina, Sami, and Edvin--from Unga Forskare, the organizers of our week-long stay. I also met the final other prize winner from the USA, Pragathi from Georgia, along with the rest of our cohort, which ranged from Will, a Ph.D. student from Australia, to college students like Jacopo from Switzerland and Yunze, my roommate for the week, from Singapore. That evening, we made the trek to a local biotech company named SciLife, where we toured molecular imaging labs and learned about prominent biomedical research centres in Stockholm. I was exhausted from travel, andbut there was no time to waste--SIYSS was beginning the very next day!

The day we arrived

Just hours later, I found myself on stage, presenting my science fair project to students from across Stockholm. I was struck by how eager my audience was to learn about my research—and, in turn, to share their own passions. Later, during poster sessions, I was interviewed by Swedish students about my work, my career aspirations, and how I first became involved in research. The highlight of the day, however, was participating in the “Minds of the Future” panel alongside Nobel Prize in Chemistry laureate Omar Yaghi. I was inspired not only by his groundbreaking work, but by his humility and his conviction that access to science must be democratized.
